Is Your Major Degree a Major Mistake?

As someone who majored in the Humanities, I believe in love at first write. Some little girls wore tutus and wanted to be ballerinas. I wanted to be the next J.K. Rowling (it seemed like the easiest way to get my Hogwarts letter at the time).

It never really occurred to me how I would make a career out of that. Nowadays it feels like being born with an affinity for language arts also means you were conjoined with an evil twin that keeps asking you, “but what do you do with that?”

According to a recent survey, that question may have an answer that surprises you. In a recent CNBC article, a survey of CEOs by the Association of American Colleges and Universities said that 74 percent of them would recommend a 21st-century liberal education in order to create a more dynamic worker. Ninety-five percent said they look for college graduates who can think clearly and solve problems and translate their ideas with good oral and communication skills.

"We want task-orientated people who have disciplines in critical thinking," stated Michael Fromm, CEO of Fromm Electric, an electrical manufacturing firm based in Reading, Pa in the article. "If someone's studied literature, they know people and have insight into themselves and customers. I find people that have a liberal arts background have a broader view of the world and will go farther in business."

Why, thank you, Mr. Fromm. There is hope for those Liberal Arts majors that want to succeed in the business world. I’d like to shake the hand of the CEOs surveyed and thank them for realizing Humanities majors can look good in suits too.

We know computer and engineering majors usually result in the highest paying positions, but communication skills are in short supply in many of these graduates - and believe it or not, that can be a detrimental factor to the bottom line of many businesses. The article continues, "In places like Google, they want people who are self-motivated and articulate and can think on their own. It's not enough just to have tech skills. Graduates have to do more."

The counterpoint to Humanities majors? They should do more, too. Just because Shakespeare didn’t have a computer doesn’t mean Liberal Arts buffs shouldn’t try their hand at technology-forward projects (I’m sure the Bard would have been an expert blogger).

The solution? It sounds to me like the key to success is being well-rounded. The best possible candidate is a diverse one that can balance the arts and the sciences in harmony to highlight a unique set of skills. If you find yourself writing poems in binary code, you’ve probably reinvented the wheel.
